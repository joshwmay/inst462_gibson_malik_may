Welcome to my golf blog! First and foremost, I am on a mission to have at least one round of golf below 90 in my lifetime, and I started this site as a way to document my 'golf journey'. My hope, is that in doing so, I will improve my game, become more knowledgable on the sport, and have something to anchor my motivation with. I will detail my thoughts and approaches to the game while marking off a bucket list of courses I plan on playing in my area.

Additionally, I will be documenting future golf outings, while reviewing places I've played, and products I have used. Since I am not the most experience or knowledgable, I will provide an email address for inquiring minds to ask questions and give commentary.

My Golf Roots

I have been hitting golf balls since I was about 10 years old, when my grandfather handed me a 9-iron and told me to go try and hit some balls in the field behind his home. I don't recall finding much success until my Pop-Pop came out and gave some guidance. The first thing I remember him explaining, was to keep my head down and focus on the back of the ball throughout the swing. He was very patient with me, and went over keeping the left arm straight and keeping the right elbow in. After those things started coming together, he attempted to teach me to let the club do the work, breathe a certain way, and let go of distractions.

I have had lessons from other instructors, but none of them have stuck with me as much as the things my grandfather taught me, and the honest truth is, the things I focus on when I play are usually the things he taught me, and any improvements I've seen are merely tweaking or reinforcing what I learned 20 years ago. . . I am however, still working on getting below 100 consistently, but the commitment is there.

How I Got Here

My relationship with golf has been inconsistent. I enjoy playing, but have had years where I play 5 or more times, and others where I did not play at all since I started. You can see by the map the different places I've played and the ones I would like to(along with links to their webpages if you're interested in booking a tee time). By far, the most enjoyable place I have gotten to play, is San Diego. The US Navy allowed me to play some unforgettable places, and San Diego offered weather I long for most times I hit the cart path back home on the East Coast.

How I got here today however, has more to do with the last 6 months. During Covid quarantine, I have had more time to rekindle things with the sport, and where I currently reside, I have found a great par-3 course and driving range. At some point, I decided to take my clubs down the road and see how my swing was doing. I was surprised that it felt mostly intact that day, but I also just enjoyed getting outside in the sun. So I started going more often until I was finding consistency, and then I decided it was time to get out and play a round.

The first round of the summer started with my brother-in-law breaking my driver by accident which had an effect on the whole day. I was not upset since I have a spare and was able to stay positive. I did not keep score, but on the 18th hole I got a birdie on a par-4. It was surprising, because I had to make a downhill breaking putt from the fringe in order to get it. That moment added some fuel to my fire, and I started focusing more on what it would take to get more birdies.

The next time I went out, I parred the first two holes of the course and was feeling pretty good before things went downhill quick; but I learned that day, that it's okay to be picky with golf partners, because another person in my party was purposely antagonizing me and intentionally talking during my backswing. Uncle or not, that is not cool, and while I can only blame myself for poor play, I felt that it was important to make note of it to avoid inviting that sort of negativity out on the course with me again(I stopped keeping score after the 4th hole out of frustration).

The next time I went out, I contacted an old friend whom I had never golfed with and hadn't seen in almost a decade. We had a lot of fun, and his style of golf meshed well with me. I did not play all that well, but I kept a legitimate score for the first time in my life and came out with a 105. After that round however, I found a pullcart because I felt that avoiding cart fees was in my interest, and that getting added exercise would be good for me. I went back and played the same course I scored 105 on 2 weeks later, but I walked it. As much as I loved the pullcart, and having instant access to things I needed, I got a 105 again. I was proud of the score though, because by hole 10, I was feeling ready to pack it in and call it a day after all the walking I had done.

Getting to the point of now, I realized that getting a memorable score in golf would require more from me than simply improving my swing consistency; I would have to improve other things about me to improve at golf. The round I walked and got a 105, my fitbit told me I finished the day with about 24,000 steps(lots of par-5's, poor play, and lack of fitness will do it). When I did make it to the 18th hole I was pretty much exhausted and the fun had evaporated for me that day, and fatigue caused me to lose focus and forget the things I had worked on for the weeks leading up.

I am attempting to get better at golf through:

  1. Improving fitness
  2. Improving consistency
  3. Boosting confidence
  4. Committing to the first 3 things
At this point, it's like I'm literally trying to become the best version of myself in order to play the best golf of my life, and there is nothing but intrinsic value to it. Hopefully doing so will set an example for my kids, and they will want to be better versions of themselves just because they see me doing it. Maybe they'll want to come walk the course with me at some point.
